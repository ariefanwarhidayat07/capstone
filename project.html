How Have Indonesia's Trade Dynamics Evolved Over the Past Three Decades?

This project investigates how Indonesia's trade dynamics have evolved over the past three decades and identifies the key factors influencing its trade balance.

  • Aims:
    • Analyze Indonesia's trade performance and its driving factors over the past three decades.
    • Identify key trade partners, commodities, and external factors influencing Indonesia’s trade balance.
    • Evaluate the effectiveness of economic policies and agreements like IJEPA.
  • Data:
    • Sources:
      • World Bank data on exports, imports, trade balance, and commodity prices.
      • UN Comtrade data on key commodities, trade partners, and bilateral trade agreements.
    • How Data Was Accessed:
      • World Bank: Data was retrieved programmatically using the World Bank API. Python scripts written in Google Colab leveraged the requests library for API queries and pandas for data processing.
      • UN Comtrade: Data was accessed via their publicly available web interface, with queries filtered to focus on key commodities and trade partners. Data was manually downloaded for analysis. While UN Comtrade offers an API for more advanced data retrieval, full access requires a subscription. For instance, the Premium Individual plan costs $2,000 per year.
    • Automation and Replication:
      • Python scripts automated data retrieval from the World Bank API, ensuring consistent data collection and processing.
      • For UN Comtrade data, manual downloads were supplemented with custom Python scripts to process and standardize the datasets for analysis.
      • All workflows and scripts have been documented in the project's Google Colab Notebooks, enabling replication with minimal manual effort.

Analytical Challenges

  • Data Cleaning and Analysis:
    • Standardizing trade data into a consistent tabular format suitable for analysis was challenging due to inconsistent formats and missing values across datasets. This was resolved using Python libraries like pandas for data wrangling and cleaning.
    • Integrating diverse datasets, such as country codes (ISO3) and HS codes for commodities, required cross-referencing multiple sources. Tools like pandas and merge functions ensured accurate alignment.
  • Data Visualization:
    • Addressing challenges in visualizing multi-dimensional data involved breaking down datasets into simpler subsets and using interactive charts to allow users to explore specific dimensions effectively.
    • Ensuring compatibility across platforms was achieved by embedding visualizations directly into web pages using Vega-Lite JSON specifications and the vega-embed library.
    • Adapting visualizations for mobile screens included:
      • Responsive Scaling: Utilized the width and height parameters in Vega-Lite to create responsive charts that automatically adapt to different screen sizes, ensuring seamless integration with mobile layouts.
      • Simplified Layouts: Simplified complex charts by dividing them into focused visualizations, highlighting key data points for better readability and user engagement.
      • Thorough Testing: Conducted rigorous testing using mobile simulation modes to verify usability and responsiveness across different devices and screen sizes.

Conclusions

  • Indonesia's trade balance has improved significantly since 2020, driven by export growth and economic recovery post-COVID-19. Key export commodities like palm oil and coal continue to dominate revenues, highlighting the country’s resource wealth but also its reliance on raw materials, which poses challenges for sustainable growth.
  • Regional and global trade partnerships remain crucial, with China, Japan, and ASEAN countries playing vital roles in Indonesia’s trade network. Singapore leads as a top ASEAN partner due to its global trade hub status, while Viet Nam's rapid industrial growth is strengthening its position as an emerging partner.
  • External risks, such as global commodity price fluctuations, heavily influence Indonesia's trade revenues, particularly in coal and palm oil. These risks underline the need for economic diversification and the development of value-added manufacturing sectors like technology, automotive components, and processed goods to reduce dependence on raw exports.
  • Trade agreements, such as the IJEPA with Japan, have bolstered export volumes and strengthened economic ties. Meanwhile, the push for renewable energy and sustainable practices offers new opportunities in green technologies, aligning with global trends toward sustainability.
  • Future policies must prioritize strengthening domestic industries and optimizing trade agreements, focusing on fostering innovation, infrastructure development, and competitiveness to ensure long-term economic stability and resilience against global market volatility.
